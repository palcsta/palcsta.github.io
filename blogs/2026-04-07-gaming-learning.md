# 🎮 Games
## What I play most
The games I return to most are Old School RuneScape and The Witcher 3. They are very different, but both are easy to stay with for a long time because they reward patience, curiosity, and attention.

OSRS is one of those games where slow progress is part of the appeal. Witcher 3 is almost the opposite: it is strong because of atmosphere, quests, and a world that actually feels worth exploring.

# 🕹️ History
## From Xbox 360 to PC gaming
A big part of my gaming history comes from the Xbox 360 era and later from PC gaming. The Xbox 360 period felt memorable because games were social, direct, and easy to start. PC gaming added something else: more freedom, mods, settings, communities, and a stronger connection between games and technology.

On PC, gaming also mixes naturally with general computer use. You end up learning about hardware, files, settings, troubleshooting, and online communities almost by accident.

# 📚 Learning
## Why educational games are underrated
Educational games are good when they do not feel like forced school exercises with a game skin on top. The useful ones teach through repetition, feedback, curiosity, and clear rewards for understanding something.

Games can teach language, problem solving, memory, planning, and even social skills. They can also make people more comfortable with experimentation, because games let you fail, adjust, and try again without making the whole process feel heavy.

# 🌍 Language
## Games can teach language naturally
One of the most practical things games teach is language. Repeated dialogue, menus, quest text, item names, and online communication make vocabulary stick much better than isolated memorization.

Even games that are not designed as language tools can still improve reading speed, recognition, and confidence, especially when you are motivated by the game itself and not only by study pressure.

# 🧠 Skills (wide)
## Problem solving and other useful habits
Good games often teach players to break big tasks into smaller ones. You learn to compare options, manage risk, recognize patterns, and stay patient when something does not work immediately. That is part of why games can be more useful than people assume.

They also teach persistence, map reading, timing, resource management, and decision making under pressure. Not every game does this equally well, but the better ones train real habits while still being enjoyable.

# ✨ View (wide, hope)
## Games are not only entertainment
I think games are at their best when they are both enjoyable and useful. They can be a hobby, but they can also help people learn, think, communicate, and explore ideas in a more active way than many passive media formats.

For me, that is part of why gaming still matters. It connects fun, memory, technology, and learning in one place.
