🤖 AI

Why AI still interests me

I am interested in AI because it can make technical work more understandable, faster to explore, and easier to improve. Used well, it is not just a shortcut for generating text or code. It can help people compare options, document decisions, review changes, and notice risks earlier.

In software delivery, that matters. AI can support testing, security review, migration planning, incident analysis, and learning new tools. I like the idea of using AI as a practical assistant inside real engineering work, especially when the goal is better quality and clearer systems.

⚙️ DevOps

DevOps is where AI becomes practical

DevOps connects many things I care about: software development, automation, infrastructure, security, and collaboration. It is also an area where AI can be useful without replacing the need for good engineering judgment.

A CI/CD pipeline is not valuable only because it runs automatically. It is valuable when it gives teams confidence, shortens feedback loops, and makes delivery more reliable. AI can help explain and improve these workflows, but people still need to design them responsibly.

🐙 GitHub

Why GitHub transformations make sense

The Eficode role focuses on GitHub adoption and migrations, which sounds interesting because GitHub is not only a place for repositories. It can become a central platform for planning, reviewing, securing, building, and releasing software.

Helping customers move from tools such as Jenkins, GitLab, or Bitbucket into a more integrated GitHub-based workflow would require both technical understanding and empathy for existing teams. That combination suits the way I like to think about technology: not only what tool is new, but how people can actually use it well.

🔐 Security

Secure delivery matters

I am especially interested in the secure and scalable side of CI/CD. Modern delivery should include testing, code review, dependency awareness, secrets handling, access control, and DevSecOps habits from the beginning.

A good Solutions Architect does not only build a pipeline that works once. The work should be maintainable, understandable, and safe enough for real organizations. That is one reason this kind of role feels meaningful to me.

🤝 Consulting

The consulting part is important

This position is also about advising customers, supporting pre-sales, and mentoring other consultants. I like that because technical work is stronger when it is connected to communication. Customers need clear options, realistic tradeoffs, and explanations that make decisions easier.

Commercial awareness also matters. A technical solution should not only be impressive. It should solve the customer's actual problem, fit their organization, and create value that they can understand. That practical side of consulting is something I want to develop further.

🌱 Fit

Why this direction would suit me

Eficode's DevOps Solutions Architect role in Finland feels like a strong match because it combines AI interest, software delivery, GitHub, automation, security, customer work, and continuous learning. It is technical, but it is not isolated from people or business needs.

The Helsinki or Tampere hybrid setup also feels practical because the work stays close to Finnish customers while still giving space for focused technical work. Direct GitHub transformation experience, pre-sales, and Finnish language skills are areas I can keep strengthening.

I want to work in a position where I can keep learning while helping others build better systems. A role focused on GitHub transformations would give me a concrete way to do that: improving developer workflows, making delivery safer, and using modern tools, including AI, with purpose.

🎮 Games

What I play most

The games I return to most are Old School RuneScape and The Witcher 3. They are very different, but both are easy to stay with for a long time because they reward patience, curiosity, and attention.

OSRS is one of those games where slow progress is part of the appeal. Witcher 3 is almost the opposite: it is strong because of atmosphere, quests, and a world that actually feels worth exploring.

🕹️ History

From Xbox 360 to PC gaming

A big part of my gaming history comes from the Xbox 360 era and later from PC gaming. The Xbox 360 period felt memorable because games were social, direct, and easy to start. PC gaming added something else: more freedom, mods, settings, communities, and a stronger connection between games and technology.

On PC, gaming also mixes naturally with general computer use. You end up learning about hardware, files, settings, troubleshooting, and online communities almost by accident.

📚 Learning

Why educational games are underrated

Educational games are good when they do not feel like forced school exercises with a game skin on top. The useful ones teach through repetition, feedback, curiosity, and clear rewards for understanding something.

Games can teach language, problem solving, memory, planning, and even social skills. They can also make people more comfortable with experimentation, because games let you fail, adjust, and try again without making the whole process feel heavy.

🌍 Language

Games can teach language naturally

One of the most practical things games teach is language. Repeated dialogue, menus, quest text, item names, and online communication make vocabulary stick much better than isolated memorization.

Even games that are not designed as language tools can still improve reading speed, recognition, and confidence, especially when you are motivated by the game itself and not only by study pressure.

🧠 Skills

Problem solving and other useful habits

Good games often teach players to break big tasks into smaller ones. You learn to compare options, manage risk, recognize patterns, and stay patient when something does not work immediately. That is part of why games can be more useful than people assume.

They also teach persistence, map reading, timing, resource management, and decision making under pressure. Not every game does this equally well, but the better ones train real habits while still being enjoyable.

✨ View

Games are not only entertainment

I think games are at their best when they are both enjoyable and useful. They can be a hobby, but they can also help people learn, think, communicate, and explore ideas in a more active way than many passive media formats.

For me, that is part of why gaming still matters. It connects fun, memory, technology, and learning in one place.

😵 Friction

Cool tech, bad feeling

AI is still one of the most impressive things I have ever seen in technology. It can generate, analyze, automate, translate, and compress hours of work into minutes.

And still, the feeling it creates is often not wonder but exhaustion. The tools are powerful, but the way they are pushed into everything makes daily life feel noisier, more synthetic, and less trustworthy.

💼 Work

More customers, not more meaning

Too much of AI today feels aimed at one goal: more customers, more clicks, more retention, more scale. It creates products, but not always meaning.

Automation should reduce useless effort. Instead, many systems are optimized to increase pressure, dependency, and constant engagement. That is one reason AI feels frustrating to me even when the underlying computer science is genuinely beautiful.

🎓 CS

My perspective as a computer science graduate

I graduated in computer science, but I still do not feel like I have a clear perspective in landing a job. I have tried to enter IT, and still I have never had the chance to actually work in it.

That creates a strange tension. We are constantly told this is the future, but for many people the future does not feel open. It feels crowded, unstable, and somehow farther away even while the technology keeps moving closer.

🔎 Trust

Research got easier, and worse

AI can summarize information extremely fast, but reliability and trust did not grow at the same speed. The discipline needed to research something properly is being damaged by the constant temptation to accept quick answers.

When everything sounds smooth and confident, it becomes harder to tell what is solid and what is just polished output. That is dangerous for students, engineers, and anyone who is honestly trying to learn.

⚙️ Automation

What automation should actually do

Automation should help people protect time, attention, and dignity. It should remove repetitive work, reduce confusion, and make real knowledge easier to reach.

If automation becomes only a machine for extracting more output from people, then something essential is lost, no matter how technically impressive the system is.

🌍 Hope

Common good over optimization

I still have hope for AI. I want it to keep improving, but in a different direction, not mainly toward effectiveness, customer growth, and endless optimization.

I want AI, computer science, and automation to move toward common good, honesty, and love for everyone on Earth. That may sound idealistic, but without that direction technology becomes empty very fast.
